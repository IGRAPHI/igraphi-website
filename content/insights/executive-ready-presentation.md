A high-stakes presentation should help decision-makers understand the issue, trust the message, and act with confidence. Most don't — not because the content is weak, but because the design is working against it.

Executive-ready is not a style. It's a structural quality. A presentation is executive-ready when it's been edited, not just assembled. When every slide has earned its place. When the most important information is the most visible information, and the reader is never left doing the work of organizing what they're seeing.

## The Difference Between Information and Communication

Presenting information and communicating a position are different activities that produce different presentations.

A presentation full of information answers the question: what do we know? A communication answers the question: what should we do, and why does it matter? Executive audiences — whether they're board members, donors, ministers, or senior agency officials — are already operating under information overload. What they need is synthesis, not more data.

An executive-ready presentation makes a clear argument. Each slide advances that argument. The structure is visible, the logic is audible, and the ask is unambiguous.

## What Executive-Ready Looks Like in Practice

**One idea per slide.** Not one topic — one idea. If you can't state the point of a slide in a single sentence, the slide is doing too much.

**Visual hierarchy that matches editorial hierarchy.** The most important number, statement, or finding on a slide should be the most visually prominent element. If everything is the same size, nothing is important. If everything is important, nothing is.

**A clean decision structure.** Executive-ready presentations are typically organized around one of three formats: a recommendation with supporting rationale, a problem with proposed solutions, or a status update with clear implications. The format should be chosen deliberately, not inherited from a previous template.

**Restrained color use.** Color in an executive presentation should mean something — this is a risk indicator, this is a trend, this is the recommended option. Decorative color adds visual noise and dilutes the signal value of intentional color choices.

## Why Most Presentations Miss the Mark

The most common reason a presentation fails to move a room is not that the underlying work is insufficient — it's that the presentation hasn't been designed for the audience it will reach. The material was assembled by someone close to the work, for an audience that isn't.

An executive audience needs context, synthesis, and a clear direction. They do not need every supporting data point, every caveat, or every intermediate step in the analysis. They need to know what matters and why, presented at a pace and density they can absorb in the time they have.

Executive-ready is about respecting the reader's attention — and designing accordingly.

## Getting There

The gap between a technically complete presentation and an executive-ready one is usually not a content problem. The information is there. The analysis is sound. What's missing is the editorial layer: the choices about what stays, what goes, what leads, and what the slide says when nothing is spoken.

That editorial layer is design work. And it's the work that determines whether a presentation informs, or whether it moves.
